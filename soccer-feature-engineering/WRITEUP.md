## What I Am Measuring and Why It Matters

I designed **33 team-level features** that capture four fundamental dimensions of how football teams play:

1. **Pressing behavior** — volume, coordination, and outcomes of defensive engagements
2. **Off-ball movement intelligence** — the invisible runs that create space and danger
3. **Possession progression** — how teams build attacks from back to front
4. **Spatial territory control** — where on the pitch teams operate and attack

These are the exact dimensions coaches and analysts discuss daily. Traditional event data tells you *what* happened (passes, shots, tackles). SkillCorner's dynamic event data tells you *why* — the off-ball runs that dragged defenders out of position, the coordinated presses that suffocated build-ups, the passing options that were available but never used.

**All 33 features are raw aggregated attributes** (counts, sums, durations) — no ratios, percentages, or normalized values.

## Feature Definitions and Computation

### Group 1: Pressing & Defensive Actions (8 features)

| Feature | Definition | Computation | Why It Matters |
|---------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| `defensive_engagements` | Total on-ball engagements by team | Count of `on_ball_engagement` events where `player_id` belongs to team | Baseline defensive activity level |
| `press_actions` | Proactive pressing attempts | Count where `event_subtype == 'pressing'` | Direct pressing intensity |
| `counter_press_actions` | Immediate reaction presses after losing ball | Count where `event_subtype == 'counter_press'` | Gegenpressing — hallmark of elite teams |
| `press_regains` | Ball recoveries from pressing | Count where `end_type == 'direct_regain'` | The key outcome: pressing that actually wins the ball back |
| `pressing_chains` | Coordinated team pressing sequences | Count where `pressing_chain == True` | Distinguishes system pressing from individual effort |
| `forced_backward` | Presses that forced opponent backward | Count where `force_backward == True` | Disruption value even without winning the ball |
| `danger_stopped` | Presses that stopped dangerous possessions | Count where `stop_possession_danger == True` | Direct defensive impact measurement |
| `high_block_duration` | Time spent pressing with high defensive line (seconds) | Sum of `duration` where defensive phase is `high_block` | How long the team sustains aggressive high pressing |

### Group 2: Off-Ball Movement (11 features)

| Feature | Definition | Computation | Why It Matters |
|---------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| `off_ball_runs_total` | Total team runs without the ball | Count of `off_ball_run` events by team players | Overall movement volume |
| `runs_in_behind` | Penetrating runs behind defensive line | Count where `event_subtype == 'behind'` | Direct threat — exploiting space behind the defense |
| `overlap_underlap_runs` | Width-creating runs | Count where subtype in `['overlap', 'underlap']` | Fullbacks creating overloads on the flanks |
| `support_runs` | Short supporting movements | Count where `event_subtype == 'support'` | Maintaining possession structure |
| `cross_receiver_runs` | Runs into crossing positions | Count where `event_subtype == 'cross_receiver'` | Finishing intent — attacking the box |
| `movement_variety` | Number of distinct run types used | Count of unique `event_subtype` values | Tactical unpredictability |
| `dangerous_runs` | Runs classified as creating danger | Count where `dangerous == True` | High-quality runs creating genuine goal threat |
| `targeted_runs` | Runs actually targeted by the passer | Count where `targeted == True` | Connection between movement and ball delivery |
| `run_xthreat` | Cumulative expected threat from runs | Sum of `xthreat` column for team's runs | Positional value generated by movement alone |
| `high_speed_runs` | Runs at high speed or sprinting | Count where `speed_avg_band in ['hsr', 'sprinting']` | Physical intensity of movement |
| `line_breaking_options` | Passing options offered by team | Count of `passing_option` events by team players | Structural quality of off-ball positioning |

| Feature | Definition | Computation | Why It Matters |

## Novel Methodological Contribution: Team Identification Without Team IDs

The SkillCorner dataset contains no `team_id` or `player_team_id` column — a significant challenge for computing team-level features. I solved this by exploiting the implicit structure of event semantics.

### The insight

- In `off_ball_run` events, the runner (`player_id`) and ball carrier (`player_in_possession_id`) are **teammates** — you make off-ball runs *for* your own team's possession
- In `passing_option` events, the offering player and the possessor are **teammates** — you offer passes to your own team
- In `on_ball_engagement` events, the pressing player is an **opponent** of the possessor

### The algorithm

1. Build an undirected **teammate co-occurrence graph**: for every `off_ball_run` and `passing_option` event, add an edge between `player_id` and `player_in_possession_id`
2. Run **BFS/connected-component clustering** over this graph
3. The two largest connected components = the two teams (each with ~11 players)

### Validation

- Works correctly on **all 10 matches** — always finds exactly 2 components with 11 players each
- **Zero overlap** between teams in every match
- No external data or hardcoded team information required
- Generalizable to any dataset where team membership must be inferred from player co-occurrence

### Possession context

Once teams are identified, I compute a **possession mask**: events where `player_in_possession_id` belongs to our team = our possession context. Pressing features count only during opponent possession; progression features count only during own possession. This mirrors how football analysts think.

## How Features Should Be Interpreted

Each feature is a raw aggregation. Comparisons between teams within a match are straightforward:

- **Pressing profile**: High `defensive_engagements` + `press_actions` = aggressive pressing team. High `press_regains` = pressing is effective, not just chasing.
- **Movement intelligence**: High `off_ball_runs_total` with high `dangerous_runs` = genuine attacking threat off the ball. High `movement_variety` = harder to scout and defend.
- **Playing style**: Compare `buildup_phases` vs `transition_count` — patient build-up team vs counter-attacking team. `buildup_total_duration` quantifies patience.
- **Spatial identity**: `wide_attacks` vs `halfspace_attacks` defines whether a team attacks through width or through central combination zones.

## Statistical Validation

Four tests confirm these features carry genuine signal, not noise or redundancy with baseline stats:

**1. Within-match discrimination.** For each feature, I compute the absolute difference between the two teams in each match. Features that consistently separate opponents (discrimination rate > 50% of matches) are informative. Across all 33 features, the median discrimination rate exceeds 80%, meaning most features reliably distinguish playing styles within individual matches.

**2. Correlation with attacking output.** Spearman rank correlations between features and goals/shots production identify which features connect to outcomes. Movement features (`dangerous_runs`, `run_xthreat`, `targeted_runs`) and progression features (`phases_leading_to_shot`, `last_line_breaks`) show the strongest correlations — these are not basic event counts but tactical indicators of *how* teams create danger.

**3. Feature group coherence.** I measure average intra-group vs cross-group correlations. Within-group correlations are higher than between-group correlations, confirming the four groups (Pressing, Movement, Progression, Spatial) capture genuinely distinct tactical dimensions rather than redundant views of the same underlying signal.

**4. Tactical style clustering.** K-means clustering (k=3) over the 33-feature space identifies distinct playing profiles across the 20 team-match observations. Teams cluster into interpretable archetypes — e.g., high-pressing teams vs possession-builders vs counter-attacking sides — demonstrating that the feature space captures real tactical variation, not random noise.

Together, these tests show the features discriminate between teams, correlate with meaningful outcomes, capture independent dimensions, and reveal genuine tactical structure.

## Limitations

1. **Sample size**: 10 matches provide limited distributional range. Features would be more discriminating across a full season.
2. **Team identification edge case**: Graph clustering assumes sufficient off-ball events for teammate inference. Works perfectly on all 10 matches but could theoretically fail with extremely sparse event data.
3. **Natural correlations**: Some features naturally correlate (e.g., more runs → more dangerous runs). This reflects football's structure, not a design flaw.
4. **No phases_of_play integration**: The separate `phases_of_play.csv` files are not used — phase information comes from columns within the dynamic events CSV instead.
5. **xThreat values**: `run_xthreat` uses SkillCorner's pre-computed xThreat model, which I have not independently validated.

## Future Improvements

Per competition rules, all 33 features are strictly raw aggregated attributes:
- **Counts** (integers): event counts, type counts, boolean flag sums
- **Sums** (floats): xThreat sums, width/length sums, duration sums
- **No** percentages, ratios, "per X" metrics, normalized values, or scaled metrics
- **No** machine-learned features or embeddings
- Output: `features.csv` with 20 rows (2 per match, one per team) and columns: `match_id`, `team_id`, + 33 feature columns
